Syllable structure

A Vietnamese syllable takes the form

\[ (C_1) (w)V (C_2|J) + T \]

where \(C_1\) is a set of initial consonants, \(w\) denotes the onglide /w/, \(V\) is a set of vowels, \(C_2\) denotes final consonants, \(J\) is the pair of offglides /w/ and /j/, and \(T\) denotes the tones. Characterising these sets and the constraints placed upon syllable formation will constitute the rest of this article.

Constraints

There are a number of constraints on syllable formation, ranging from simple restrictions to ad-hoc syllables that simply don't appear because they sound unnatural (or perhaps causation is the other way round). We have met one such case already (the non-existence of /əw/ or “ơu” in an otherwise full table) but there are a number of others, such as the inexistence of unnatural-sounding rimes like “ơng” or “uốp” or “ưm”. My intention here is to give the most reasonable set of simple restrictions that covers all but the ad-hoc instances.

  1. Tone constraint.If a syllable ends in /p/, /t/ or /k/, its tone must be sắc or nặng.
  2. Offglide constraint.If a syllable ends in /w/, its vowel must be front or central. If a syllable ends in /j/, it vowel must be central or back.
  3. Short vowel constraint.A syllable cannot end with the vowel ă or â. It must have a following offglide or consonant.
  4. Onglide /w/ constraint. If a syllable has the onglide /w/, its vowel must be front or central [and not start with /ɨ/].

Syllables again

A syllable is of the form \[ \textsf{Start} \times \textsf{On-glide} \times \textsf{Vowel} \times \textsf{End} \times \textsf{Tone} \] where
  • Start = {•, B, C, CH, D, Đ, G, H, KH, L, M, N, NG, NH, PH, R, S, T, TH, TR, V, X}
  • On-glide = {•, W}
  • Vowel = {A, Ă, Â, E, Ê, I, O, Ô, Ơ, U, Ư, IÊ, ƯƠ, UÔ}
  • End = {•, W, J, C, P, T, M, N, NG}
  • Tone = {NGANG, SẮC, HUYỀN, HỎI, NGÃ, NẶNG}
  • • denotes the absence of a sound
  • W and J denote the sounds /w/ and /j/ in IPA respectively—in English, “w” and “y”. All other letters represent their usual sound
  • “qu” is just C + W
  • Words ending in -anh, -ach, -inh, -ich, -ênh, -êch are considered phonemically -eng, -ec, -ing, -ic, -eng, -ec respectively.
